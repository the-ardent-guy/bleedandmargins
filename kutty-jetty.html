The Field.

What the research showed

India's kidswear market is large, growing, and almost entirely talking to the wrong person. Most brands address parents — comfort, safety, durability, washing instructions. All valid. All necessary. And all deeply uninteresting to a five-year-old who has opinions about what they want to wear. Kutty Jetty's brief was to build a brand that worked on both levels — reassuring enough for the parent paying, exciting enough for the kid wearing. The colour research was where everything began.

01 Colour is the first language of children's fashion — and most brands get it wrong.

Research into children's colour psychology and South Indian visual culture showed a consistent pattern: kids respond to colour with full conviction. They don't hedge. The muted, "tasteful" palettes most premium kidswear brands use are chosen for parents who want inoffensive Instagram grids — not for kids who want to run into a room and be noticed. Kutty Jetty's palette was built from how South Indian festivals, textiles, and streetlife actually look. Saturated. Joyful. Unapologetic.

02 South Indian identity in kidswear was almost entirely absent as a genuine design position.

The Indian kidswear market had two dominant modes: international brands with no regional identity, and traditional ethnic wear with too much of it. The space in between — a brand that was distinctly South Indian in spirit without being a costume — was wide open. "Kutty" is what every paati calls her grandchild. It's what every amma calls her child when they do something brilliant. The name was already embedded in the culture. It just needed a brand around it.

03 Export-quality fabric at accessible prices was the functional gap nobody had filled locally.

Tirupur — 40 km from Coimbatore, producing 90% of India's cotton knitwear exports — was making world-class fabric that mostly left the country. The same quality going into European children's clothing was available to an Indian brand willing to work with local mills. Kutty Jetty took that material seriously: pre-washed, breathable, built for all-day movement. Not fast fashion for kids — properly made things that last and can be passed down.

The Brief

Build a South Indian kidswear brand that kids actually want.

Kutty Jetty came with the product already figured out — export-quality South Indian mill cotton, fun prints, unisex fits, festive and everyday collections. What needed building was everything around it: the brand identity, the colour system, the Shopify storefront, and the visual language that would make it feel like its own thing rather than a regional version of something else.

The brief was to get into kids fashion deeply — understand what colours work, what prints land, how South Indian visual culture could inform a brand that felt contemporary rather than costumey. Make it regional without making it nostalgic. Make it playful without making it childish. And make it honest: these are good clothes, made well, from the right place.

The Insight

The kutti knows what they want. Design for them first.

Every other brand was designing for the parent's confidence. We designed for the kid's joy. The parent buys it; the kid wears it — and if the kid doesn't want to wear it, nobody wins. South Indian kids grow up surrounded by the most saturated colour palette in the world: kolam patterns, temple gopurams, silk sarees, festival lights. They have excellent taste. We just had to meet them where they already were.

The brand system was built from that logic outward. Colour wasn't an afterthought — it was the first decision. Typography that felt bold and fun without screaming. A name in Tamil script alongside the English. A brand that didn't explain its South Indian-ness, it just was.

The Work.

02 Deliverables
01

Brand Identity & Colour System

Built the brand from colour up — because in kidswear, colour is the first conversation. Deep research into South Indian visual culture: kolam geometry, lungi stripes, festival saturations, temple palettes. Six primary hues named after things South Indian kids actually know — Festival Red, Mango, Jade, Turmeric, Deep Ink, Sky. Print directions drawn from the same source: kolam-inspired geometry, festive dots, lungi stripes, banana leaf motifs. Regional without being a costume. Bold without being loud.

Brand Identity Colour Research Print Direction Typography Tamil Script Integration
02

Shopify Storefront — kuttyjetty.com

Built a storefront that carried the brand's energy — warm, vibrant, South Indian without being self-conscious about it. Product photography direction led with the colour and the movement: kids in clothes, clothes being worn, not clothes on white backgrounds. Festival and everyday collections clearly delineated. Size guides and material callouts — pre-washed, export cotton, unisex — positioned as genuine features, not afterthoughts. The kind of site that makes a parent feel good about buying and makes a kid want what's in it.
